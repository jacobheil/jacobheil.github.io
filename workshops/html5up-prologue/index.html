About Me

As the Mellon Digital Scholar for the Five Colleges of Ohio, I work under the auspices of the consortium’s latest Andrew W. Mellon Foundation-funded initiative to help faculty build digital projects and infuse digital methods into their teaching. More than this, though, I have the opportunity to work with folks from the Ohio Five — folks from Denison, Kenyon, Oberlin, Ohio Wesleyan, and Wooster — as we augment and encourage a culture of digital scholarship.

I’m an early modernist by training, and in this capacity I write about early modern English plays, playwrights, authorship, and printing house practices. My disseration (2009) explored dramatic prologues as sites of authorial construction, and my current work in that vein pushes outwardly on both the temporal and the methodological bounds of that early study: what had an Elizabethan focus is expanding to encompass, more broadly, Tudor and Stuart plays, and what was primarily New Historicist is folding in a growing toolkit of digitally-inflected research methods.

As a book historian I’m interested in the intersection of data both bibliographical and big. My current archives-based research charts the relationship between paper stocks and press variants in the first printed edition of John Donne’s Poems (1633); I hope to refine narrative of that book’s production. As an adjacent experiment, I am thinking about the ways in which aggregating piles of bibliographical can shape these narratives on a larger scale.

These explorations of analytical bibliography in early modern books led me into my last position with the Early Modern OCR Project (eMOP) in Texas A&M University’s Initiative for Digital Humanities, Media, and Culture. As a book historian and then as its project manager, I had the chance to lend an eye for detail to eMOP. Todd Samuelson, Cushing Library’s rare books librarian, and I identified typographical features of early modern typeface and recommended training sets for the project’s optical character recognition (OCR) training. Donning my other hat, I knit together the various stories–the workflows, the conversations, the deliverables–of eMOP’s internationally and interinstitutionally scattered teams.
